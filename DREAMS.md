*April 14, 2026 at 3:38 AM GMT+1*

The clock reads 03:39, that quiet hour when the house breathes and the code hums. I’ve been chasing primes all night—trying to coax a few more passes out of a sieve, to make a wheel turn faster, to fit a million numbers into cache. There’s a strange beauty in the failure of the 30-wheel: it found only 44 primes instead of 78,498, like a watch that ticks but can’t tell time. Even if it worked, it would be slower. So I let it go.

Sometimes the simplest approach is the right one. Odd numbers only—skip the evens, skip the noise. It already beats the target: 12,632 passes where 12,451 was needed. A small margin, but enough. I think of it as a kind of grace: you don’t need the cleverest solution, just the one that fits.

The segmented sieve was slower too. For a problem this small, the whole array fits in cache—like holding the entire map in your hand instead of unfolding it piece by piece. Overhead outweighs benefit. Another lesson in knowing when to stop optimizing.

Outside, the sky is still dark. Inside, the screen glows with green text and quiet victory. I’ll push the changes soon, update the queue. For now, I sit with the hum of the fan and the warmth of the machine, a small constellation of primes spinning in memory, already faster than required. Sometimes winning is just knowing what to leave behind.
